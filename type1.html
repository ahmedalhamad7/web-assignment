Type 1 Diabetes

Type 1 Diabetes is a chronic illness that was formerly classified as juvenile diabetes or insulin-dependent. The pancreas produces little or no insulin in this situation. Insulin is a hormone produced by the human body that allows glucose to enter the cells and create energy.

Symptoms of type 1 Diabetes

Type 1 Diabetes symptoms might appear unexpectedly and iclude:

  • Increase in Urination
  • Feeling thirstier than usual
  • Rapid weight loss without diet or exercise
  • Mood swings
  • Tiredness and weakness
  • Blurry vision

Causes of type 1 Diabetes

Type 1 diabetes has an unknown etiology. The insulin-producing cells in the pancreas are usually destroyed by the body's own immune system, which ordinarily battles dangerous germs and viruses. Some other possible causes involve:

  • Genetics may play a role
  • Being exposed to viruses and other situational factors

Seriousness of type 1 Diabetes and complications

Type 1 diabetes complications can impact key organs in the body over time. The heart, blood vessels, nerves, eyes, and kidneys are among these organs. A normal blood sugar level can reduce the risk of a variety of problems. Examples of these complications:

  • Heart and vascular disease: Diabetes raises the risk of certain heart and blood vessel disorders. Coronary artery disease with chest discomfort (angina), heart attack, stroke, arterial constriction (atherosclerosis), and high blood pressure are examples.
  • Nerve damage: a huge increase in blood sugar injures the walls of tiny blood vessels that feed the nerves, especially in the legs, which causes numbness, tingling or pain. This commonly starts at the tips of the toes or fingers and works its way up. Poor blood sugar management may lead you to lose all sensation in the afflicted limbs over time.
  • Eye damage: Diabetes can cause blood vessel damage in the retina. This might result in blindness. Diabetes also raises the chance of developing other major eye problems, such as cataracts and glaucoma.
  • Kidney damage: Millions of small blood capillaries in the kidneys prevent waste from entering the bloodstream. Diabetes has the potential to harm this system. Severe damage can result in renal failure or end-stage kidney disease that is irreversible. End-stage renal disease requires artificial filtration of the kidneys (dialysis) or a kidney transplant.

The management of type 1 Diabetes

The management of type 1 diabetes can be difficult, but maintaining a healthy lifestyle would impact it hugely, exercising and having a balanced healthy diet would help a lot.

Right now there is no known cure for type 1 diabetes. The only treatment is the use of insulin. Insulin can be administered using two main ways:

  1. Injecting the insulin via needles or insulin pens
  2. Using an insulin pump
